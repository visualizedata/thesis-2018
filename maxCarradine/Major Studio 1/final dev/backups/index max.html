Perpetual Extremes

The term "extreme weather event" typically conjures thoughts of blizzards or hurricanes. A Google image search of "extreme
weather NYC" will show you multiple pages of what you would imagine: apocolyptic flooding, or twisted metal carnage from
Hurricane Sandy. Thorough destruction, implied death.

But New York City regularly experiences a different extreme weather event not shown in our search results. Heat waves (defined
as three or more days above 90 degrees Farenheit) have started occurring with disturbing frequency and duration due to an
increasingly unforgiving urban heat island. And some population groups live with higher risk than others.

These are the longest heatwaves in NYC's history since 1895

It can be hotter inside during a heat wave


This unique study tracked indoor temperatures within a southeast-facing apartment
(without AC) during August 2016. Some believe they're taking refuge from the heat by
staying indoors but that's not always the case. Even when it cools down outside,
walls can trap heat inside subjecting occupants to higher ambient temperatures.

Source: Harlem Heat Project

12.5% of NYC Seniors Lack AC at Home


Senior citizens are the most vulnerable population during a heat wave, especially those
without AC at home. The city does provide free air conditioners to low-income seniors, but
many aren't able to assume the utility bills.

And even though cooling centers are available throughout the city, this low-mobility
population is more inclined to stay at home.

Learning from Chicago


The 1995 Chicago heat wave is equally known for its high death toll and poor response
by city officials. Over 650 people died, and the iconic images of refrigerated mobile
morgues brought in to handle the overflow of bodies seemingly shamed city officials into
overhauling their emergency response plan. A similar heat wave in 1999 yielded a far lower
death toll. This was primarly due to the city providing free and direct transportation to cooling
centers and having police officers go door-to-door and dispatching EMTs when necessary.

NYC currently does not include these practices in their heat emergency response plan.
