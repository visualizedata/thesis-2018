Strength in Numbers: Mapping U.S. Naval Diplomacy, 2012-2018


Global seapower competition is intensifying. As China dredges up ≈3,200 acres of territory ex nihilo in the South China Sea and emplaces sea missiles astride the Philippine archipelago, and as Russia disperses its submarines more widely across the Mediterranean as it conducts simulated attack strafing runs against NATO vessels in the Baltic Sea, the Obama and Trump national security teams have faced increasingly intricate naval diplomacy and maritime security challenges in recent years.

With these shifts in mind, this thesis examines the overall patterns in U.S. naval diplomacy worldwide from 2012 to 2018, querying diplomatic tension in the STRATFOR dataset under both the Obama and Trump administrations and comparing them to vessel movements as well as freedom of navigation operations (FONOPS). In addition, this thesis examines the geostrategic impact of Secretary of State Hillary Clinton’s diplomatic pivot to Asia in 2011 and Secretary of Defense Leon Panetta’s decision in 2012 to move 60% of the U.S. fleet to the Pacific, breaking with decades of a roughly even 50%-50% Atlantic-Pacific force posture.

Increased Mediterranean Presence

In recent years, U.S. carrier activity in the Mediterranean has intensified, with more pronounced activity east of Suez (near Europe) than west, in the Middle East. With Russia dispersing its submarines more widely across the Mediterranean, "[c]arrier strike groups [like the USS Harry S. Truman and USS Carl Vinson, for instance, that would just transit through the Mediterranean at maximum speed to get to the Middle East are spending much more time in the Mediterranean, not only contributing to [Syria and Iraq overflights in] Inherent Resolve, but also working the Russian presence problem in the Eastern Mediterranean, Black Sea," and other regions.

Net result: ▲ 32% increase in U.S. naval activity in the Mediterranean in the most recent reporting period

7 U.S. FONOPs in 6 years

According to Adm. Philip Davidson -- the U.S. Pacific Commander -- “China is now capable of controlling the South China Sea in all scenarios short of war with the United States.” In order to avoid that high-stakes scenario in Oceania, the U.S., Australia, and other regional allies in the ASEAN region have been conducting freedom of navigation operations (FONOPs) in and around the South China Sea, directly challenging China's assertion of sovereignty and instead asserting the right for all ships to sail through the region.

At the same time, it is worth remembering Secretary of Defense Robert Gates' warning that "if Chinese highly accurate cruise and ballistic missiles, anti-ship cruise and ballistic missiles, can keep our aircraft carriers behind the second island chain in the Pacific, you’ve got to think differently about how you’re going to use aircraft carriers."


Net result: ▲ 83% increase in U.S. naval activity in the South China Sea (SCS) in the most recent reporting period
